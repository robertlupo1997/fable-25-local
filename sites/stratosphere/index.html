High Altitude Platform Station · Radiosonde Package · 4.8 kg · 2000g latex envelope

A 2,000-gram balloon, inflated with 1.8 m³ of hydrogen, strains against its tether in the pre-dawn dark. At 0600 UTC, release. The atmosphere — all 39 km of it — waits above. The physics will do the art direction.

01 — Cloud Deck

1,500 m · T = +2.3°C · P = 845.6 hPa

The sky closes.

The cumulus deck — base at 900 m, tops at 2,200 m — forms a solid ceiling as seen from below. Rising through it takes 4 minutes. Grey-white diffusion. Then, abruptly, a different world.

Ascent rate: ~5.2 m/s
Visibility: <100 m (inside deck)
Lapse rate: −6.5 K/km (moist adiabatic)

02 — Cloudtops

5,500 m · T = −21.0°C · P = 505.0 hPa

The top of the weather.

The cloud layer extends to the horizon in every direction — a white continent 100 km across. Below it: rain, low pressure, three European countries. Above: absolute clarity. Half the mass of Earth's atmosphere now lies beneath the payload.

Cloud tops: 2,800 m average
Altocumulus: visible at 4,500 m
Solar flux: unattenuated at this altitude

03 — Upper Troposphere

10,000 m · T = −49.9°C · P = 264.4 hPa

Where aircraft cruise.

The jet stream tears past at 65 m/s — invisible, but real, shaking the payload string. At this altitude, commercial airliners fly within 300 m of us. Cirrus wisps trail overhead. Temperature: −49.9°C, computed exactly by the ISA lapse rate of 6.5 K per kilometre.

Jet stream: 60–70 m/s, 200–300 hPa layer
Cirrus ceiling: 11,000 m above MSL
Balloon diameter: ~2.8 m (expanding)

04 — Tropopause

12,000 m · T = −56.5°C · P = 193.3 hPa

The lapse rate zeros.

At 11,000 m, the atmosphere stops cooling. Temperature stabilises at −56.5°C — the coldest point of the journey. Above this boundary, the stratosphere begins. Weather systems cannot cross it; convection terminates here. The sky is visibly darker.

Temperature: −56.5°C (isothermal layer begins)
Pressure: 193.3 hPa (19% of sea level)
All weather: below this line

05 — Lower Stratosphere

18,000 m · T = −56.5°C · P = 75.9 hPa

The sky turns ink.

The Rayleigh scattering column is thinning fast — exponentially, with scale height 8,500 m. Only 18% of the atmosphere remains above. Blue photons scattered to your eyes grow scarce. The zenith is deep indigo. Stars are minutes away.

Column density above: ~18% of sea level
Rayleigh scale height: 8,500 m
Balloon diameter: ~5.1 m (still expanding)

06 — Ozone Layer

23,000 m · T = −52.4°C · P = 40.5 hPa

Ten parts per million.

The ozone layer spans roughly 20–35 km, peaking near 25 km with concentrations of 10 ppmv. Invisible, odourless. It absorbs 97–99% of the sun's UV-B radiation. Without it, surface DNA mutates on exposure measured in seconds. We are inside it now.

O₃ concentration: ~8–10 ppmv peak
Absorption: 97–99% of UV-B (280–315 nm)
Column depth: ~300 Dobson units

07 — Near Space

32,000 m · T = −46.5°C · P = 8.7 hPa

Stars at noon.

Pressure: 8.7 hPa — less than 1% of sea level. The sky has surrendered its blue. Stars are visible in daylight. The horizon has curved. The atmosphere, in its entirety, is a thin blue film draped across the belly of the visible world below.

Pressure: 8.7 hPa (0.86% of SL)
Density: 0.0133 kg/m³ (1.1% of SL)
Balloon diameter: ~8.4 m

09 — Burst

39,000 m · T = −24.9°C

The envelope fails.

A crack propagates at 340 m/s through the overstressed latex. In 0.03 seconds, the 10-metre envelope is shreds. The payload, suddenly weightless in free fall, begins to drop. The parachute is slaved to a barometric trigger — deployment at 28 km. Descent rate: 7.3 m/s average. Return ETA: 48 minutes.

Burst trigger: stress limit of latex at 10 m diam
Chute deploy: 28,000 m barometric trigger
Landing zone: 68°N 23°E (pred. 3.1 km drift)

10 — Descent

~20,000 m falling

The sky returns.

The atmosphere thickens around the descending payload. Blue re-enters the zenith. Cloud layers re-appear — first the altostratus, then the cumulus tops. Pressure rises. Temperature rises (stratospheric warming on descent through isothermal layer). At 2,000 m, ground contact is 10 minutes away.

11 — Recovery

0 m · T = +14.8°C · P = 1013.1 hPa

Ground.

The payload lands in a birch forest 68°N 23°E — 2.8 km from the predicted point. Total mission duration: 2 hours, 7 minutes. The radiosonde has sampled temperature, pressure, and humidity at 39,000 data points. Kiruna receives the telemetry. The atmosphere has been measured, once more, from inside.

Mission duration: 2h 07m
Data points: 39,000+ (1/sec)
Max altitude: 38,947 m

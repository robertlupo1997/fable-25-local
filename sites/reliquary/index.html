I

Do not touch · Ref. 1923-VII-004

The Klein Reliquary

Ref. No. 1923-VII-004
Materials Blown glass, theoretical topology, silver filigree (18th cent.)
Dimensions 14 × 14 × 28 cm  ·  Interior volume: undefined (see Conservator's Report, 1987)

Acquired from the estate of Professor H. Lindemann, Munich, 1923, who described the object as "a wine flask from a region I visited only once." The vessel has no interior or exterior surface — qualities which rendered the original acquisition classification as Liquid Vessels technically inaccurate. The silver mounts were added in 1887 by a craftsman whose workshop notes describe the commission as "straightforward, though the customer asked that we not discuss what goes inside." Attempts to fill the vessel have returned results consistent with the topology. Currently stored under modified atmospheric conditions at the recommendation of the Physics department, who declined to elaborate in writing.

Condition note, 2019: Stable. A hairline fracture observed in 1954 has not propagated, nor has it been possible to determine which side it is on.

II

Do not touch · Ref. 2003-XII-017

Penrose Bronze

Ref. No. 2003-XII-017
Materials Lost-wax cast aluminium, structural impossibility
Dimensions 23 × 23 × 23 cm  ·  Each interior angle: 90°  ·  Triangle sum: 270°

Anonymous donation, received without accompanying paperwork in December 2003. The donor did not wait for a receipt. The object presents as a triangular form in which each of three corners meets at a right angle, which is geometrically impossible in Euclidean space; total interior angle in any triangle is 180°. No deviation from Euclidean space has been detected in the surrounding gallery. Visitors are advised that sustained observation of the object may cause temporary certainty in propositions that are demonstrably false. The museum accepts no liability for conclusions reached while standing closer than 0.5 metres.

Condition note, 2024: Patination consistent with age and handling. The object has been rotated by security staff without incident, though each staff member independently reports that it looked "correct" from whichever angle they chose.

III

Do not touch · Ref. 1978-IV-031

Shadow No. 7 (With Absent Caster)

Ref. No. 1978-IV-031
Materials Photons (attenuated), cotton ground substrate, structured absence
Dimensions Shadow extent: 42 × 38 cm  ·  Caster dimensions: not applicable

Isolated from a construction site in Hamburg in 1978, following complaints from site workers who could not locate the object responsible. Subsequent investigation by the Physics department confirmed the presence of a stable, well-defined shadow and the absence of any occluding body at any altitude above the ground plane. The shadow has remained stable in position and form since acquisition, drifting only slightly (±3 cm) over seasonal cycles in a manner consistent with changes in ambient light source angles — though no light source has been identified that would produce the observed azimuth. The museum displays the shadow under controlled lighting conditions that do not, in any conventional sense, produce it.

Condition note, 2022: Stable. Night-staff have reported the shadow is present after dark. This has not been investigated further at the request of the night-staff.

IV

Do not touch · Ref. 1956-IX-002

Faceted Tesseract (Fragment)

Ref. No. 1956-IX-002
Materials Hypercubic zircon, fourth-dimensional geometry, standard 3-dimensional space
Dimensions Observed faces: 24  ·  Calculated faces: 8  ·  Discrepancy: stable

Found in the estate of a theoretical physicist, 1956, whose papers contained only the note: "do not store near other geometric forms — they become confused." The object is a four-dimensional hypercube expressed partially in three-dimensional space. Its sixteen vertices and thirty-two edges are fully present and measurable; its eight cubic cells are visible only in rotation, wherein they appear as twenty- four faces. The object rotates continuously in four dimensions. Visitors observing the rotation for more than ninety seconds are advised to sit down afterwards and allow their spatial intuition to recover before operating heavy machinery.

Condition note, 2018: Excellent. The object has acquired no wear despite continuous rotation since 1956, consistent with the absence of friction in the fourth dimension.

V

Do not touch · Ref. 1981-II-055

The Ascending Fragment (Penrose Stairway, Section IV)

Ref. No. 1981-II-055
Materials Carved limestone, continuous ascent, unresolved causation
Dimensions Rise per step: 18 cm  ·  Total height: indeterminate  ·  Direction of travel: both

Excavated from an unnamed monastery in the Bavarian Alps in 1981. The acquisition team reports that the removal was straightforward, but that no member of the team felt they had finished descending the staircase by the time they reached the loading dock. The stairway ascends continuously: each step rises eighteen centimetres above the previous, and the twelfth step connects to the first, which is lower. The monastery, when contacted in 1982, confirmed that "the staircase has always been like that" and offered no further elaboration. An identical fragment descends perpetually. They are the same fragment, viewed from two positions that cannot simultaneously exist.

Condition note, 2023: Wear pattern on treads is consistent with very heavy foot traffic. Museum staff have been requested to stop testing this.

The Conservatory

A collection of living light

Founded in 1907 in the cloud forests above Mindo, Ecuador, the Glasswing Conservatory houses 214 live species whose wing colour is generated entirely by nanostructure — no yellow pigment, no blue dye, no melanin gradient. Only geometry, measured in hundreds of nanometres.

The butterflies you see here belong to a lineage that abandoned pigmentation entirely some forty million years ago. What replaced it was more durable and more astonishing: a layered chitin film whose thickness alone determines which wavelengths of light reinforce and which cancel. Tilt your head and the blue shifts to green. Angle of view is the palette.

Optical Physics

How a wing becomes a spectrum

A single wing scale — one-tenth of a millimetre across — carries a stack of alternating chitin and air lamellae. Light striking the top surface reflects partially; the remainder enters, reflects from the lower surface, and rejoins the first beam. The two beams have now travelled different distances.

Optical path difference OPD = 2 · n · d · cos θ n ≈ 1.38 (chitin refractive index) d = 120 – 480 nm (film thickness) θ = angle of incidence

When OPD equals a whole-number multiple of a wavelength, the two beams add constructively: that colour blazes. When OPD equals a half-multiple, they cancel. Each RGB channel — 650 nm, 550 nm, 435 nm — is evaluated independently, producing a colour that depends entirely on d and θ.

Film thickness varies from the base of the wing (≈ 120 nm, scattering violet and blue) outward to the margin (≈ 480 nm, where red and green alternately reinforce to yield gold and copper). Every individual produces a slightly different thickness profile — no two specimens are identical.

Per-wavelength reflected intensity I(λ) = ½ · (1 + cos(2π · OPD / λ)) R = I(650 nm) · G = I(550 nm) · B = I(435 nm)

Plan Your Visit

Open to the public since 1923

The Conservatory sits at 1,650 metres elevation on the slopes of Pichincha, an hour north-west of Quito. Mornings — before 10 am — are cool and still: the ideal conditions for wing display. Afternoon cloud rolls in from the Pacific and the butterflies retreat to the canopy.

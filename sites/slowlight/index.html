ON LONG EXPOSURE

The photograph
as a clock

A photograph is a record of light that arrived. The shutter opens, and everything that moves becomes a trace — a smear of probability across silver halide crystals, a memory the paper holds long after the shutter closes.

Long exposure flips the relationship between time and image. A single second freezes motion. Four hours of open shutter traces the rotation of the Earth itself — each star a bright scratch arcing around the celestial pole. The star knows nothing of being photographed. The film remembers everything.

Every exposure on this page is computed the same way: additive accumulation, frame by frame, never erased. Nothing is drawn whole. The trails are real because they were earned, one photon at a time.

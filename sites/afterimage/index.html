BAYER 4×4 · 1-BIT · LIVE

ORDERED
DITHERING

The eye invents gradients from grids.

Before digital screens learned to lie, every halftone was a negotiation. Print machines couldn't output grey — only ink or no ink, presence or absence. So they cheated, cleverly, by distributing black dots across a regular threshold matrix.

Ordered dithering uses a predetermined grid of thresholds applied at each pixel. The Bayer matrix — invented by Bryce Bayer at Kodak in 1973 — arranges these thresholds so that quantization error disperses with maximum spatial uniformity. Sixteen positions. Sixteen chances to be wrong in exactly the right way.

What you see is not grey. It is the suggestion of grey, produced by your visual cortex averaging a mosaic of binary decisions your brain is too slow to count individually.

RECIPE
BAYER 4×4 MATRIX:
 0  8  2 10
12  4 14  6
 3 11  1  9
15  7 13  5

FOR EACH PIXEL (x, y):
  L = luminance(source[x, y])
  T = bayer[x mod 4][y mod 4] / 16
  OUT = (L > T) ? WHITE : BLACK

RESULT: 1-bit. No grey. All grey.
SPREAD 02 / 05

CMYK
HALFTONE

Four separations. One impossible image.

Commercial printing uses four inks: Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Key (black). Each is screened separately, rotated to a different angle so their dots don't stack. Cyan at 15°. Magenta at 75°. Yellow at 90°. Black at 45°. Align them. Misregister them slightly. Rosette patterns bloom.

The process color system assumes the eye will blend the dots into continuous tone. It was correct. The brain does the compositing. You supply the model, and the model supplies you in return.

This is why the zine looks different every time you copy it — each generation the halftone screen re-registers against the previous dots, and Moiré interference adds its own texture to whatever truth the image still holds.

RECIPE
RGB → CMY:
  C = 1 − R
  M = 1 − G
  Y = 1 − B
  K = min(C, M, Y)

HALFTONE DOT (channel, angle θ):
  uv_rot = rotate(uv, θ)
  cell = fract(uv_rot × density) − 0.5
  dot = length(cell) < √channel × 0.5

COMPOSITE: paper − C·cyan
                 − M·magenta
                 − Y·yellow
                 − K·black

RADIAL LENS FRINGE · RGB SPLIT · LIVE

CHROMATIC
ABERRATION

Every lens forgets where it came from.

Glass bends different wavelengths of light by different amounts. Red refracts less than blue. Where the lens is thick — near the frame edges — dispersion accumulates. The image the lens forms for red is not quite the image it forms for blue. At the periphery, colours separate. Fringes appear.

Camera makers have fought this since Galileo. Achromatic doublets. Apochromats. Floating elements. Computational post-correction. But the ghost persists, and we have learned to read it as authenticity — proof that the photograph was taken through glass, by a machine, in a moment now gone.

This shader shifts each channel radially from centre — red outward, blue inward — by an amount that grows as the square of the distance from the image midpoint. Imperfect. Honest.

RECIPE
FOR EACH PIXEL (x, y):
  dir = normalize(uv − centre)
  dist = length(uv − centre)
  str = dist² × aberrationScale

  R = sample(uv + dir × str).r
  G = sample(uv           ).g
  B = sample(uv − dir × str).b

  OUT = vec3(R, G, B)
SPREAD 04 / 05

VIDEO
FEEDBACK

Point a camera at its own monitor.

In 1974, Steina and Woody Vasulka aimed a video camera at the television screen it was feeding. The result — a tunnel of recursive images, each delayed, each smaller — became foundational to video art. The machine dreaming of itself.

What unfolds is not a mirror. Each iteration adds the noise and bandwidth limits of the recording chain. Colours shift. Contrast clips. Geometry bends. The feedback loop is not stable — it is a dynamical system, sensitive to initial conditions, drifting toward strange attractors that look like mandalas and like nothing at all.

Here the "camera" and "monitor" share one framebuffer: each frame is rendered by slightly zooming, rotating, and tinting the previous frame, then compositing a faint new signal on top. The tunnel opens.

RECIPE
INIT: framebuffer A ← 0

EACH FRAME:
  B = sample(A, zoom 1.015)
        rotated +0.3°/frame
        × decay 0.97

  B += new_signal(t) × 0.10

  DISPLAY B
  SWAP A ↔ B

SOBEL 3×3 · GLITCH LAYER · LIVE

EDGE
DETECTION

Only the borders were ever real.

Irwin Sobel's 1968 operator was designed for robot vision: give a machine a way to find where one thing ends and another begins. Two 3×3 kernels, one horizontal and one vertical, measure the luminance gradient across neighbouring pixels. Where the gradient is steep — where the image changes quickly — there is an edge.

What remains after edge detection is a phantom of the original: a skeleton of transitions, a diagram of where attention should go. Cognitive scientists argue this is closer to what the primary visual cortex actually encodes. You see edges first. The interior fills in after — a guess, an inference, an afterimage.

The glitch layer is procedural: scanlines that skip, bit-shift artifacts creeping from the left margin, horizontal tears drifting with time. Signal degradation as aesthetic choice.

RECIPE
SOBEL KERNELS:
  Gx: [−1  0 +1 | −2  0 +2 | −1  0 +1]
  Gy: [−1 −2 −1 |  0  0  0 | +1 +2 +1]

FOR EACH PIXEL:
  gx = convolve(luma, Gx, 3×3 window)
  gy = convolve(luma, Gy, 3×3 window)
  edge = √(gx² + gy²)

GLITCH: jitter rows by sin(y×hash+t)
        at stochastic row intervals

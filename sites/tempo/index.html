The Drift

Reich's signature move: pull one voice barely out of phase. The ghost rhythms that emerge — accents that appear and vanish, phrases no performer intended — are what he called phase music.

On rhythm and time

When independent rhythms cycle at ratios of 3 to 4 to 5, the beats never fully align — except at the precise beginning and end of every sixty-unit period. In between, their interference creates patterns: accents that migrate across the measure, silences that breathe in unexpected places, phrases that emerge from the mathematics rather than from any single performer's intention.

The composer Steve Reich encountered this while working with tape loops in the 1960s. He aligned two identical recordings, then let one drift. The music that resulted from that drift — not composed, only initiated — changed his career and, ultimately, the way a generation came to listen.

"I don't push the music, I set it in motion and listen to where it goes."

01

We witness what most people never see.

The Institute of Atmospheric Light maintains a continuous record of auroral events above the Arctic Circle. Our photometric arrays, riometers, and fluxgate magnetometers feed data to an international archive that has tracked every significant geomagnetic storm since 1987.

What you see in the sky is not magic. It is a collision: solar-wind electrons spiraling down Earth's magnetic field lines into the upper atmosphere at 100–150 km altitude, exciting oxygen atoms into their 557.7 nm emission. Green light. And higher — above 200 km — nitrogen bands emit violet and magenta. That is the signature of a deep storm.

Our job is to record it precisely. The sky is a laboratory. We take notes.

03

The Tools of Patience

0123 4567 89
  1. 01

    Fluxgate Magnetometer

    Three orthogonal sensors measure Earth's magnetic field to 0.1 nT resolution. H-component deflections map ionospheric current systems in real time. Bay disturbances on the x-trace signal substorm onset within minutes.

  2. 02

    All-Sky Camera

    A 180° fisheye lens captures the entire auroral oval from horizon to horizon at 20-second cadence. Dawn keogram synthesis reveals the night's full structure as a single time-compressed image.

  3. 03

    Riometer Array

    Cosmic radio-noise absorption at 30 MHz measures energetic particle precipitation in the D-region ionosphere — the invisible companion to optical emission, penetrating cloud cover and polar night alike.

Ultramarine

Beyond the Sea

Na₈₋₁₀Al₆Si₆O₂₄S₂₋₄ · PB29

Shadow OKLCH Luminance Scale · Hue 264° · C 0.22 Tint

The word itself maps a trade route. Ultra mare — beyond the sea — because the lapis lazuli that yields this color comes from a single valley in the Hindu Kush, the Kokcha River drainage of Badakhshan, and had to cross the Mediterranean before it could become paint.

The pigment was ground by hand from stone threaded with pyrite gold: powdering the lapis, kneading it in warm wax and oils, then working it in water to separate the deep lazurite from the pale calcite. It took months. It cost accordingly.

Medieval patrons held tight control. A 1408 altarpiece commission from Siena specifies that ultramarine — purchased at the patron's expense, not the painter's — shall be used only for the Virgin's mantle. No lesser subject warranted the price. When Vermeer died in 1675, leaving debts and eleven children, the estate listed unpaid ultramarine purchases among the primary liabilities.

Synthetic ultramarine arrived in 1826, when Jean-Baptiste Guimet won the prize offered by the Société d'Encouragement pour l'Industrie Nationale — 6,000 francs for a process to replicate the mineral at 1/40th the cost. The chemistry produces the same lazurite structure: sodium, aluminum, silicon, and the S₃•⁻ radical, a sulfur trimer that absorbs at 590–610 nm and returns blue. But geography was removed. So was the difficulty. Painters began using twice as much.

  • DiscoveredNatural: antiquity (Afghanistan)
    Synthetic: 1826, France
  • Source mineralLapis lazuli (lazurite)
    Badakhshan, Afghanistan
  • Color IndexPB29 (synthetic)
    PB31 (natural)
  • LightfastnessExcellent · ASTM I
  • Notable usersVermeer, Raphael
    Bellini, Monet
  • Absorption peak590–610 nm (S₃•⁻ radical)

II Room 2 of 6

Vermilion

The Red of Life and Death

HgS · PR106

Shadow OKLCH Luminance Scale · Hue 27° · C 0.23 Tint

Mercury and sulfur, heated together, give red. Mercury(II) sulfide — HgS in its red crystalline alpha form — has been mined at Almadén in Spain and Idrija in Slovenia since antiquity, and ground to powder in every civilization that found it.

The Chinese were using cinnabar lacquerware by the second millennium BCE. Roman women powdered it on their cheeks. The Byzantine chronicler Theophanes wrote that emperors signed documents in "purple ink" that was, in fact, vermilion. The color sits exactly where orange becomes red: warm enough to suggest fire, stable enough to suggest blood.

Medieval manuscript illuminators treasured it for initials and miniatures, though they knew it darkened. The darkening is photochemical: bright light converts the stable red α-HgS to dark gray β-HgS. Old masterworks sometimes show gray vermilion passages beneath their glazes — the cost of that warmth, compounded over centuries.

Cadmium red replaced vermilion for most studio uses by 1900. Vermilion now survives in conservation, in specialist printing, and in the memory of a specific warmth that cadmium, however brilliant, does not quite replicate.

  • DiscoveredNatural: 2nd millennium BCE
    China, Rome, Mesoamerica
  • Source mineralCinnabar (HgS)
    Almadén, Spain; Idrija, Slovenia
  • Color IndexPR106
  • LightfastnessPoor · photodegrades to β-HgS (gray)
  • Notable usersTitian, Raphael
    Byzantine icon painters
  • ToxicityHighly toxic · mercury compound

Viridian

The Cool Eye

Cr₂O₃·2H₂O · PG18

Shadow OKLCH Luminance Scale · Hue 163° · C 0.12 Tint

Some colors arrive with a founding story. In 1838, a Parisian colormaker named Pannetier exhibited at the Salon a new green — chrome oxide hydrate, Cr₂O₃·2H₂O — made by a process he had discovered and declined to publish. A competitor, Guignet, independently worked it out by 1859.

Its virtues are formal. Viridian is transparent — it glazes without muddying. It is cool — the hue sits on the blue-green edge, closer to cyan than to the warm greens of landscape. And it is permanent: no fading, no chemical interaction with other pigments, no darkening. In the 1870s and 1880s, the Impressionists needed exactly this: a reliable, transparent cool green that could render shadow in a world of unmixed sunlight.

Monet's water lily canvases — dozens of them, made over twenty years of failing eyesight — depend on viridian for the depths beneath reflected sky. His late work grew more abstract as his cataracts worsened, but the cool green of submerged darkness remained, almost a fixed coordinate in the pictorial world he refused to relinquish.

The color asks to be used carefully. Unmixed, it looks almost artificial. Painters who have understood it — Cézanne, Sargent, Sickert — deploy it as a quiet note: a shadow coolness, a tonal underpinning that never draws attention to itself. Its gift is restraint.

    Published process: Guignet, 1859
  • ChemistryChromium(III) oxide hydrate
    Cr₂O₃·2H₂O
  • Color IndexPG18
  • LightfastnessExcellent · ASTM I
    Chemically inert
  • Notable usersMonet, Cézanne
    Sargent, Sickert
  • CharacterTransparent · cool · no tinting strength

IV Room 4 of 6

Ochre

The First Mark

Fe₂O₃ + clay · PY43

Shadow OKLCH Luminance Scale · Hue 65° · C 0.15 Tint

The oldest pigment in this collection is also the simplest: iron oxide mixed with clay, the rust color of the earth itself. At Blombos Cave in the Western Cape of South Africa, archaeologists found engraved ochre pieces dated to 75,000 years before the present — not yet painting, but the first known human manipulation of a material for its visual properties.

At Lascaux, ochre is horse. At Altamira, it is bison. At hand-stencil sites from Sulawesi to Argentina, it frames the negative space of human presence. The pigment predates language by an order of magnitude. It may predate symbolic thought in the modern sense, or it may be our oldest evidence of it. The debate is ongoing and, perhaps, unresolvable.

Iron oxide is stable in a way that more sophisticated pigments are not. It does not fade, does not react, does not darken. Yellow ochre (goethite, FeO·OH) converts to red ochre (hematite, Fe₂O₃) when heated — you can make red paint from yellow paint by burning it. Cave painters knew this. The knowledge was not recorded; it simply persisted, in the hands, across ten thousand generations.

What ochre knows, and what the more refined pigments sometimes forget, is that color is not an achievement. It is a given. The earth comes ready-colored. The question is what you do with what you find.

    ~75,000 BCE
  • ChemistryGoethite (yellow): FeO·OH
    Hematite (red): Fe₂O₃
  • Color IndexPY43 (yellow ochre)
    PR102 (red ochre)
  • LightfastnessExcellent · ASTM I
    Permanent in all media
  • Notable sitesLascaux, Altamira
    Sulawesi, Chauvet
  • Thermal shiftYellow → red at ~260°C
    (goethite → hematite)

Tyrian Purple

The Whelk and the Crown

C₁₆H₈Br₂N₂O₂ · Dibromoindigo

Shadow OKLCH Luminance Scale · Hue 336° · C 0.17 Tint

To make this color, you killed shellfish by the thousand. The Murex brandaris and Hexaplex trunculus — sea snails endemic to the Mediterranean — each contain a small gland that produces dibromoindigo, a molecule that, when exposed to light, shifts from pale yellow-green to a purple that does not fade in salt air or strong sun.

The city of Tyre, on the coast of what is now Lebanon, controlled the trade. Contemporary accounts describe the processing yards by their smell — the decomposing murex is historically documented as one of the more oppressive industrial odors of antiquity. A single gram of dried pigment required between 10,000 and 12,000 snails. This was not inefficiency; it was simply the chemistry. The dye gland is small, the yield is small, and the color emerges only at the intersection of the right enzyme, the right substrate, and sunlight at the right moment.

Roman law eventually formalized what economics had already established. Tyrian purple was the color of power. To wear it without permission was to claim proximity to the emperor. The phrase born in the purple — porphyrogennetos in Greek — named imperial children born in the Porphyra, the purple-draped chamber where empresses labored. When Constantinople fell in 1453, the specific knowledge of production was lost.

Modern chemistry recovered the molecule in the early twentieth century. The snail industry did not return. What returned was the formula — and the realization that the color still cannot be cheaply made.

Lead White

The Ground of Everything

2PbCO₃·Pb(OH)₂ · PW1

Shadow OKLCH Luminance Scale · Hue 85° · C 0.003 Tint

Every other pigment in this museum is a color. Lead white is a ground — the substrate, the beginning, the light that everything else is painted against.

From the Renaissance through the nineteenth century, almost every European oil painting began with lead white. Not as a color choice but as a structural material: basic lead carbonate (2PbCO₃·Pb(OH)₂) dries faster than any other white in oil, builds impasto that does not crack, and develops a slight translucency over time that allows light to penetrate the paint film and return. Rembrandt's late self-portraits, with their loaded brushwork and their battered surfaces, are lead white. The thick, sculptural quality of his brushstrokes is not possible without it.

The pigment was made by the Dutch process: strips of lead rolled into pots with vinegar, buried in horse dung, left for months. The acetic acid and carbon dioxide from the decomposing organic matter converted the lead surface to lead carbonate. Workers breathed the dust. The symptoms of chronic lead poisoning — fatigue, wrist drop, cognitive fog — were common enough in paint-grinding workshops to be considered occupational risk.

Lead white was banned in residential paint in most European countries by the 1920s, and in the United States by 1978. Artists' lead white — flake white — remains available under strict labeling requirements, used by painters who know the structural properties and accept the responsibility. Nothing else builds quite the same light. The impasto of Van Gogh's sunflowers is zinc and titanium white. The surfaces are beautiful. They are also different.

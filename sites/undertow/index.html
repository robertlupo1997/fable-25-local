The Network

Four active systems, three oceans

Every data packet you send crosses at least one of these routes. They rest on the abyssal plain six kilometres beneath the surface, armoured near shore and bare fibre in the deep — where the pressure alone is enough to kill any submersible not built for it.

ATLANTICA-7
SEGMENT B-4

Landing Station

The cable exits the conduit beneath a parking lot in Lisbon. From here it descends the continental shelf at 8°. Within six kilometres, the seabed is 200 metres below you.

Twilight Boundary

At 1,000 metres, no sunlight has reached the sediment. The polyethylene sheathing is rated to 8,000 atm. Crossing the Charlie-Gibbs Fracture Zone now — a 350-kilometre gash splitting the Atlantic floor.

Abyssal Plain

The floor levels to within 40 centimetres per kilometre. It is the flattest terrain on Earth. The cable rests unsupported on 65 million years of compressed foraminifera. Nothing will disturb it here.

Field Incident Registry

Damage, by cause

Since 1858, submarine cables have been cut, crushed, bitten, dragged, and buried. Most repairs take three weeks. The ocean does not acknowledge urgency. Sharks, in particular, are drawn to the electromagnetic field generated by the power conductor — a side effect the industry noted, then quietly stopped publicising.

Measured Latency

Terminus to terminus

Light in glass travels at roughly 200,000 km/s — about two-thirds the speed of light in vacuum. The distance does the rest. Transatlantic: 68 ms round-trip. Transpacific: 135 ms. The ocean is not the bottleneck.

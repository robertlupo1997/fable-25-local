Every line on a seismogram is a record of the planet's internal life. This essay draws one line per earthquake — each spike the amplitude of ground motion felt at the instrument, each decade a chapter in a still-unfinished story.

The data comes from the USGS Earthquake Hazards Program, drawn directly from their FDSN Event Service. 413 earthquakes of M7.5 or greater, occurring between January 1926 and December 2025, baked into this page at build time. earthquake.usgs.gov ↗

A note on completeness: seismic instrumentation improved dramatically through the twentieth century. Events before 1960 are likely undercounted — particularly in remote ocean regions. What the early decades show is a lower bound, not a full picture.

Felt Report

  • M9.5 1960 May 22 Great Chilean Earthquake 25 km depth · shallow The largest earthquake ever recorded. The rupture zone extended 1,000 km along the Valdivia fault. Tsunamis reached Japan (6 m), Hawaii (10 m), and the Philippines. Earth's free oscillations were measured for the first time.

Felt Report

Felt Report

  • M9.1 2011 Mar 11 Tōhoku, Japan 29 km depth · shallow The most powerful earthquake in Japan's recorded history. The tsunami reached 40 m in some coastal areas; nearly 20,000 killed. The Fukushima Daiichi nuclear plant was destroyed by the wave. Japan moved 2.4 m toward North America.

What the record shows

Great earthquakes are not random in time. They cluster: the 1952–1965 "age of great earthquakes" produced six events above M8.5 in thirteen years, including the two largest ever measured. The 1980s, by contrast, passed with no M8.1 events at all — whether a genuine seismic quiet or a catalog artifact remains debated.

No region is immune. Circum-Pacific subduction zones dominate, but the Assam–Tibet collision, the intraplate Balleny Islands, and the oceanic Wharton Basin show that the planet's skin is under stress everywhere it is being recycled, bent, or born.

The next great rupture will happen. The only uncertainty is where, and when the instrumentation will be in place to catch it whole.
